How are Dining Options distributed across Boston?

An analysis of restaurants focusing on transit time, price, and quality. All from the perspective of a Northeastern student.

Observations 2,590

Team Noura Alami, Evelyn Fabel, Colin Jennings
01 Overview

Topic & Research Tasks

Dining and eating out is a core component of student life, yet access to affordable, quality food is perhaps unequal around universities, and more broadly, urban environments. This project explores the restaurant landscape of Boston and how their locations are distributed in relation to the Northeastern campus.

We had three questions guiding our reserch focus:

  • Where are restaurants relative to Northeastern?
  • Does rating correlate with number of ratings?
  • Which neighborhood has the most diverse dining options?

By examining these patterns, we aim to support students in eating better when going out. By focusing on transit time as a core variable, we address the fact that most students lack car access. The visualizations may help a user find that hidden gem restaurant, or it may highlight an entire neighborhood that might may have previously looked out of reach. This project may also help identify gaps in access that may affect student well-being.

02 Data

Dataset Overview

Data was collected from the Google Places API using a broad set of restaurant-related keywords to capture as many venues across Boston as possible. The raw results were then filtered by radius and by establishment type — keeping only restaurants, cafes, bars, and bakeries — which reduced the dataset from 3,106 to 2,590 entries, each with 12 features.

03 Visualizations

Exploring the Visualizations

Interactive Map of Boston Restaurants
Altair
Interactions

The map shows all restaurants located across Boston and surrounding neighborhoods, with circle colors representing the price category. Use click-and-drag and zoom to navigate around the map. Hover over any marker to see the restaurant name, and click to bring up a tooltip with additional info. From the map, it is clear that restaurants are most densely concentrated around Northeastern's campus and the density begins to thin out towards outer neighborhoods. Neighborhood Popularity by Transit Time

Interactions

The bubble chart plots each of the top 15 Boston neighborhoods by their average transit time from Northeastern on the x-axis and their average review count on the y-axis, with bubble size reflecting the total number of restaurants in that neighborhood. 04 Findings and Conclusion

What We Learned

01

Restaurant density is highest near Northeastern's campus, with neighborhoods like Fenway, Back Bay, and the South End containing the largest concentration of dining options. Students have no shortage of choices within a short transit distance.

02

Price level does not appear to be strongly tied to proximity to campus. Budget, mid-range, and upscale restaurants are distributed across all transit distances, meaning students on any budget can find options nearby without needing to travel far.

03

Review count seems to correlate with proximity. Restaurants closer to campus tend to have significantly more reviews, suggesting that student traffic meaningfully drives online engagement. Future work could explore whether this reflects actual visit frequency or simply greater awareness of nearby spots.
