How are Dining Options distributed across Boston?

An analysis of restaurants focusing on transit time, price, and quality. All from the perspective of a Northeastern student.

Observations 2,590
Features 11
Team Noura Alami, Evelyn Fabel, Colin Jennings
01 Overview

Topic & Research Tasks

Dining and eating out is a core component of student life, yet access to affordable, quality food is perhaps unequal around universities, and more broadly, urban environments. This project explores the restaurant landscape of Boston and how their locations are distributed in relation to the Northeastern campus.

We had three questions guiding our reserch focus:

  • Where are restaurants relative to Northeastern?
  • Does rating correlate with number of ratings?
  • Which neighborhood has the most diverse dining options?

By examining these patterns, we aim to support students in eating better when going out. By focusing on transit time as a core variable, we address the fact that most students lack car access. The visualizations may help a user find that hidden gem restaurant, or it may highlight an entire neighborhood that might may have previously looked out of reach. This project may also help identify gaps in access that may affect student well-being.

02 Data

Dataset Overview

Data was collected from the Google Places API using a broad set of restaurant-related keywords to capture as many venues across Boston as possible. The raw results were then filtered by radius and by establishment type — keeping only restaurants, cafes, bars, and bakeries — which reduced the dataset from 3,106 to 2,590 entries, each with 12 features.

03 Visualizations

Exploring the Visualizations

Interactive Map of Boston Restaurants
Altair
Interactions

The map shows all restaurants located across Boston and surrounding neighborhoods, with circle colors representing the price category. Use click-and-drag and zoom to navigate around the map. Hover over any marker to see the restaurant name, and click to bring up a tooltip with additional info. Use the slider at the top to filter by maximum travel time from Northeastern, and use the drop-down to filter by establishment type.

Takeaway

From the map, it is clear that restaurants are most densely concentrated around Northeastern's campus and the density begins to thin out towards outer neighborhoods. Budget-friendly options are widely distributed across the city, making affordable dining accessible regardless of where students are located. Predictably, there are fewer restaurants outside the city center, but by using the transit time slider, you can see how many far-away places suddenly become accessible due to them being located close to transit hubs. This highlights how important public transport can be in discovering new places.

Distribution of Transit Times to Restaurants
Altair
Interactions

Hover over each bar to reveal a tooltip with the transit time range, count, and price category for that bin. Stacked bar colors correspond to price category.

Takeaway

If you are willing to travel more than 15 minutes, a huge number of restaurants become options due to neighborhoods like Fenway, Downtown, and Back Bay. However, after 45 minutes of transit time, there are diminishing returns. The takeaway of this graph is that there is some sweet spot of travelling where the most number of restaurants become available, and that is not too far from campus. Another takeaway from this graph is the distribution of restaurant pricing: the vast majority of restaurants are classified as $$, with $$$ and $$$ being quite rare. Since these ratings are self-described by the restaurant, this may be a response to consumer demand for mid-level prices.

Neighborhood Popularity by Transit Time
D3
Interactions

The bubble chart plots each of the top 15 Boston neighborhoods by average of transit time to restaurants from Northeastern on the x-axis and their average review count on the y-axis, with bubble size reflecting the total number of restaurants in that neighborhood. Hover over any bubble to see exact transit times, average review count, and total restaurant count for that neighborhood.

Takeaway

Downtown has the highest restaurant count with 195 locations, while neighborhoods like Fenway and Back Bay show higher average review counts relative to their transit time, suggesting stronger student engagement with nearby dining options. Neighborhoods requiring longer transit times seem to show smaller bubbles and lower review counts. This is likely a function of proximity to the center of the city: Northeastern is located near busy neighborhoods in the center of the city. As you move further outside the center of the city, and thus also further away from campus, restaurants tend to have less reviews.

Rating vs Review Count
Altair
Takeaway

This plot shows the average number of reviews for a restaurant of each rating, shown by the dashed line. The blue highlight is the 95% confidence interval of this mean. This shows that rating does, in fact, correlate with review count. Higher rated restaurants tend to have more reviews. Interestingly, it starts dropping off after 4.5 stars. This suggests that the very highly-rated restaurants may seat less people. It also suggests that very few restaurants are able to hold on to near-perfect ratings of 4.8 - 5.0 stars, and as they accumulate reviews, they will generally normalize.

Restaurant Ratings and Price by Neighborhood
Altair
Interactions

Clicking on any neighborhood's box reveals a heat map showing distribution of restaurant pricing and rating for the neighborhood. This chart shows the distribution of ratings across the top 15 Boston neighborhoods by restaurant count, sorted from most (Downtown at 195) to fewest restaurants (West Cambridge at 40), with box color reflecting mean price level.

Takeaway

Rating distributions are fairly tight across all neighborhoods, with medians between the highest and the lowest only 0.2 stars away. It may be hard to draw generalizations from the overall quality of food in each neighborhood, but the distribution from the entire boxplot tells you that there are highly rated spots in every neighborhood. The average price (number of $) is encoded by the color of the boxplot. It suggests that Back Bay and South Boston Waterfront (Seaport) are the most expensive neighborhoods, while Jamaica Plains, Chinatown, and Roxbury are less expensive. The heatmap shows that in many neighborhoods, there is heavy concentration of restaurants in the $$ category in the 4.0 to 4.5 star range. This reinforces ideas from the histogram (that the majority of restaurants fall in the $ or $$ category) and from the scatter plot of reviews, which suggests that mode of rating is 4.4 stars.

04 Findings and Conclusion

What We Learned

01

Restaurant density is very high near Northeastern due to its central location and proximity to trendy neighborhoods like Back Bay, Fenway, and Chinatown. Consequently, a majority of restaurants in Boston and Cambridge are accessible within 40 minutes of travel, but even 20 minutes of travel by public transport yields more than a hundred options. This highlights the versatility of public transit for students looking to eat out; there are a huge number of restaurants in new neighborhoods to discover if you familiarize yourself with the various subway lines and buses.

02

Building on the previous point, review count seems to correlate with proximity: restaurants in neighborhoods close to campus tend to have significantly more reviews. While these neighborhoods are already popular, this also suggests that student traffic meaningfully traffic. Universities like Northeastern, Boston University, Wentworth, Simmons, and Harvard Medical are all located close to each other and contribute to Boston having a student population over 150,000. These students interact with restaurants frequently and are contributing factors to nearby neighborhoods being popular.

03

Generally, higher-reviewed restaurants have more reviews. This suggests that well-reviewed spots may be a good choice. Restaurants that are good tend to stay in business longer in order to accumulate more reviews. The highest-reviewed restaurants have less ratings on average. This suggests that they may be smaller establishments seating fewer people and providing a more personalized experience. It also suggests that restaurants cannot be expected to maintain a perfect rating forever. As they accumulate more reviews, the rating generally normalizes.

Future Work

Future work could build on takeaway #2 and focus even more on the student aspect. We could determine if review count reflects actual visit frequency or simply represents greater awareness of nearby spots. We do not have data as to how active students are in reviewing restaurants, or what percent of customers leave a review or return. Our metric for popularity was simply average review count, so it could certainly be refined through other data sources. Further work might require surveying students to determine their most-visited restaurants and whether they had reviewed them or shared good experience through word of mouth alone. This could be compared with the online presence of restaurants to see if more ratings actually means more students, or whether students prefer not to submit online ratings. Through more comprehensive data collection, this project could be adapted to address university students better and perhaps generate a "must try" restaurant list at every price range.
